Research Statement: Personalized Cancer Chemotherapy

Chemotherapeutic responses in cancer are strongly influenced by DNA mutations within tumor cells. Gene mutations can disrupt key cellular pathways and alter treatment outcomes. Most approved chemotherapeutic agents fall into three major categories: DNA replication inhibitors (e.g., Gemcitabine, Hydroxyurea), DNA repair inhibitors (including DNA-PK, NHEJ, and HR inhibitors), and modulators of histone modifications triggered by the DNA damage response. By understanding how these mutations interact with drug-induced cellular stress, it becomes possible to develop targeted therapies with greater sensitivity and improved safety profiles — overcoming many of the limitations associated with current chemotherapeutic agents.

Drug response can vary widely depending on mutation-driven gain- or loss-of-function alterations in cancer cells. For example, mutations in TP53 — one of the most frequently mutated tumor suppressors in human cancers — can promote resistance to chemotherapy. Similarly, excessive single-stranded DNA formation may enhance homologous recombination (HR), resulting in resistance to Poly(ADP-ribose) polymerase (PARP) inhibitors currently used in BRCA1/2-mutant breast cancers.

Additional mutations affecting DNA repair factors — including HR, non-homologous end joining (NHEJ), and DNA replication pathways — may also promote drug resistance through mechanisms such as: compensatory DNA repair activity, R-loop accumulation, oncogene hyperactivation, impaired apoptosis, altered ubiquitin-ligase function, and dysregulated metastatic signaling. Collectively, these adaptations weaken the effectiveness of conventional chemotherapeutic strategies.

“Personalized chemotherapy begins with decoding mutation-driven vulnerabilities within each tumor.”

Current chemotherapy regimens for cancers such as lung cancer, breast cancer, and cholangiocarcinoma still rely heavily on broadly acting agents like gemcitabine and etoposide, without sufficient molecular precision. In contrast, personalized cancer medicine — including mutation-guided therapies and selective immunotherapies — represents the future of oncology.

The long-term goal of this research is to develop systems capable of classifying tumor-specific mutations in individual patients and predicting optimal drug combinations before treatment is initiated. Such precision tools will support clinical decision-making, extend patient survival, and reduce toxicity to healthy tissues — advancing the broader vision of personalized chemotherapy and precision oncology.
