Research Areas

In my independent research at Kyoto University Hospital, I uncovered a novel tumor suppressive mechanism in B-lymphomas: internal CXCL13 expression leads to CXCR5 receptor internalization, disrupting essential germinal center migration and triggering lymphoma apoptosis. This intrinsic pathway, driven by a distal super-enhancer on the CCNG2 locus, reveals a previously unknown mechanism of natural B-lymphoma suppression. Further, I demonstrated that aberrant CXCL13-CXCR5 signaling in solid tumors (e.g., HCT116, HepG2) enhances expression of cell cycle regulators (PTP4A1), metastatic receptors (CCR7), and ubiquitin ligases involved in the p53 degradation, contributing to drug resistance via p53 dysregulation. This approach led to the identification of CCR7, PTP4A1, and p53-specific ubiquitin ligases as novel therapeutic targets, particularly in the context of CXCL13 and co-expressed CXCR5 signaling.

Project Title: Epigenetic Regulation and Chromosome Conformation in Meiotic DNA Double-Strand Break Formation and Repair

Institution: Osaka University, Japan

During my doctoral studies at Osaka University, I developed a strong foundation in chromosome biology, with a particular focus on the molecular mechanisms governing DNA double-strand break (DSB) formation and repair during meiosis. Using budding yeast meiosis as a model system, I investigated how chromatin structure and transcriptional activity influence meiotic recombination.

My research addressed the interplay between epigenetic regulators, cohesin complexes, and the transcriptional machinery that together shape meiotic chromosome architecture into loop–axis domains. I identified the RNA polymerase II elongation complex Paf1C as a central regulator of histone modifications, including H2BK123 ubiquitination and H3K4, H3K36, and H3K79 trimethylation, which are critical for higher-order chromatin organization.

Importantly, I demonstrated that Paf1C regulates the spatial distribution of programmed DSBs by promoting Spo11-dependent DSB formation at recombination hotspots while suppressing breaks at cold spots (Gothwal et al., Genetics, 2016). This mechanism safeguards genome inheritance by preventing chromosomal mis-segregation, aneuploidy, and genome instability.

These findings have broad relevance to chromosomal disorders such as Down syndrome and Klinefelter syndrome, which arise from defective meiotic chromosome segregation. In cancer biology, mammalian homologues of Paf1C and SET1 methyltransferases are frequently overexpressed, with MLL1 fusions being common in childhood leukemia and several solid tumors.

Building on this foundation, my current research examines the role of WDR5, a core component of the MLL1 complex, which is highly expressed in glioblastoma and other solid cancers. Aberrant transcription driven by these chromatin regulators promotes oncogenic signaling and alters proteasomal regulation of high-fidelity DNA repair pathways, particularly homologous recombination.

Collectively, these studies inform the rational design of chemotherapeutic strategies targeting histone methyltransferase–overexpressing tumors, offering a promising avenue toward more sensitive and precise cancer therapies.

Postdoctoral Training: Chromosome Translocations and B-Lymphomagenesis

Mentor: Prof. Tasuku Honjo (Nobel Laureate in Physiology or Medicine, 2018)

Institution: Kyoto University, Japan

During my postdoctoral training under Prof. Tasuku Honjo, I expanded my research into site-specific recombination in B cells, focusing on the molecular mechanisms by which Activation-Induced Cytidine Deaminase (AID) induces DNA double-strand breaks (DSBs) at immunoglobulin (Ig) loci during antibody class-switch recombination (CSR). While CSR is essential for antibody diversification, it also poses inherent risks of chromosomal translocations and genome instability.

My work centered on elucidating how the histone acetyl-reader protein BRD2 regulates the repair of AID-induced DSBs. I demonstrated that BRD2 promotes non-homologous end joining (NHEJ) while suppressing homologous recombination (HR) and microhomology-mediated end joining (MMEJ), thereby biasing repair toward the canonical pathway used during CSR (Gothwal et al., Nucleic Acids Research, 2024).

Mechanistically, BRD2 coordinates DSB repair by recruiting cohesin loaders such as NIPBL, the architectural protein CTCF, and other chromatin regulators to the Ig locus, ensuring efficient and spatially constrained NHEJ. However, this same architectural framework also facilitates pathogenic IgH–MYC chromosomal translocations, thereby contributing to B-lymphomagenesis.

These findings revealed a dual role for BRD2: safeguarding genome integrity during physiological antibody diversification while simultaneously enabling oncogenic rearrangements under dysregulated conditions. Notably, mutations in NIPBL—classically associated with Cornelia de Lange Syndrome (CdLS)—may also impair humoral immune responses, highlighting broader links between chromatin architecture, immunity, and disease.

Beyond its role in CSR, BRD2 emerged as a potential oncogenic driver in B cells. Nevertheless, germinal center (GC) reactions deploy intrinsic tumor-suppressive checkpoints that eliminate B cells bearing non-functional or oncogenic receptors. Building on this observation, subsequent projects explored how these checkpoints restrict affinity maturation and prevent GC exit of potentially malignant clones.

Importantly, bromodomain proteins such as BRD2 are also actionable therapeutic targets. Small-molecule inhibitors, including JQ1, have demonstrated efficacy in suppressing BRD-dependent oncogene expression. Collectively, this body of work establishes BRD2 as a critical regulator of genome stability and a promising target in B-cell malignancies.

The germinal center (GC) reaction is a central process in the humoral immune response, enabling B cells to undergo somatic hypermutation and antibody class-switch recombination, ultimately generating high-affinity antibodies. Upon antigenic stimulation, GC B cells upregulate two key regulators: BCL6, which promotes B-cell survival and proliferation, and activation-induced cytidine deaminase (AID), which introduces programmed DNA double-strand breaks (DSBs) to drive antibody diversification.

While AID activity is indispensable for immune adaptation, it also poses a substantial oncogenic risk. Off-target DNA breaks can give rise to chromosomal translocations such as IgH–MYC and IgH–BCL6, which are hallmark lesions of Burkitt’s lymphoma and diffuse large B-cell lymphoma (DLBCL). In addition, malignancies such as B-cell chronic lymphocytic leukemia (B-CLL) and multiple myeloma arise from mutated B cells that have passed through memory or plasma cell differentiation stages.

Despite lifelong and repeated activation of GC reactions in humans, these lymphomas remain relatively infrequent. This paradox strongly suggests that germinal centers harbor robust intrinsic quality-control mechanisms capable of identifying and eliminating potentially malignant B cells before malignant transformation occurs. Consequently, GC-derived B-lymphomas represent a powerful biological system for uncovering endogenous tumor suppression mechanisms.

Working Hypothesis

I hypothesized that the germinal center microenvironment possesses an inherent capacity to spontaneously eliminate B-cell lymphomas arising from replication stress and AID-induced genomic instability.

To identify such tumor-suppressive mechanisms, I employed the Raji cell line, a well-established human Burkitt’s lymphoma model, and exposed it to 4 mM hydroxyurea (HU) to induce DNA replication stress analogous to that experienced during GC reactions. This stress-based screening strategy was designed to mimic physiological GC checkpoints that operate under conditions of intense proliferation and DNA damage.

I then performed single-cell RNA sequencing in combination with chromatin immunoprecipitation (ChIP) targeting H3K4me3, a histone modification associated with transcriptionally active promoters. This integrated approach enabled high-resolution mapping of transcriptional and epigenetic responses to replication stress.

This analysis revealed stress-responsive gene programs encompassing multiple druggable gene families, including zinc finger transcription factors, E2/E3 ubiquitin ligases, DNA repair regulators, and G-protein–coupled receptors. The coordinated regulation of these pathways suggests the existence of a conserved transcriptional network that governs survival versus elimination decisions in stressed GC B cells.

Importantly, because many of the identified genes encode pharmacologically tractable proteins, this GC-based screening strategy highlights a rational framework for discovering novel chemotherapeutic targets directly from physiological tumor-suppression programs.

Impact & Future Direction

My future research will focus on defining how these candidate genes regulate tumor suppression versus tumor progression. This work identifies previously unrecognized mechanisms for eliminating pre-malignant B cells and uncovers novel therapeutic targets with potential relevance to both hematological malignancies and solid tumors. Ultimately, these findings support the development of stress-informed, precision-targeted cancer therapies.

Identification of MBD1 as a novel therapeutic target (Kanazawa University as an independent researcher)- I developed a unique approach using Raji cells to screen novel tumor suppression mechanisms. Raji is a germinal center (GC) derived-B-lymphoma. The GC-derived B-lymphomas are naturally suppressed in GCs, thus they could be informative on tumor suppressor mechanism if induced with genotoxic-stress, similar to B cells experience in GC microenvironment. I treated Raji with Hydroxyurea (HU), simulating a genotoxic stress as faced by GC B cells i.e. DNA-replication stress. Among several induced genes in HU-induced genotoxic stress in Raji cells MBD1, a gene encoding for DNA-CpG methylation-binding protein was induced. Functional analysis revealed that MBD1 suppresses genomic instabilities at the BCL6 locus, preventing BCL6 rearrangements. BCL6-translocations are observed in 31% of diffuse large B-cell lymphoma (DLBCL) and other cancers (Gothwal et al., 2024, In Preparation). MBD1 also inhibits IRF4 and PRDM1, key regulators of plasma B cell differentiation, suggesting its role in suppressing multiple myeloma (a plasma B cell malignancy). Furthermore, MBD1 depletion sensitized Raji cells to HU, gemcitabine, and etoposide, highlighting its potential as a therapeutic target in cancers resistant to DNA replication inhibitors.
